On Imaginary Moneys

Tim's website
2020-04-18
blockchain, cryptocurrency, economics, money, philosophy
| 7 minutes read

Just yesterday, I got my hands on a really wonderful graphic novel called "The Thrilling Adventures of Lovelace and Babbage". It's about the invention of the first computer, the so called analytical engine. It starts by telling the story of Ada Lovelace's coming of age. And, in particular, about her being deliberately pushed into mathematics and kept away from fulfilling any kind of creative purpose. That, of course, doesn't go well for too long, before Ada starts learning about "imaginary" numbers. A concept, so seemingly close to poetry, her mother becoming worried that Ada could become like her dad. A mad polyamorous poet?

Anyways, apart from the stories about Ada and her companion Charles Babbage, there's lots of mathematical concepts being nicely animated in comic style. And so there's also this part where Ada's imagination quite literally starts blossoming when she realizes that numbers do not only exist on a single dimension. The book visualizes this really beautifully by Ada moving along the scale of real numbers, soon realizing that she can carefully climb up imaginary stairs. She climbs carefully, but at one point she misses on and falls "back into reality". Yes, I know, the book is pretty cool.

I just wish, I had the imagination of Ada, back in college (or was it high school?), when my teachers desperately tried to explain me the concept of imaginary numbers. I mean it was not that I didn't want to understand, or that I was lazy or not interested. It was just that this whole topic didn't make much sense to me. Why have imaginary numbers when all you need in the world is real numbers. After all, there's no imaginary sheep you need to count, and no imaginary apples you can pick. And so the concept of imaginary numbers actually never stuck with me. Funnily enough, when we learned about vectors later, I didn't have any trouble following along.

Maybe, it's that concept of telling a story, of opening up the imagination, that ultimately allows us to learn and be truly creative. And with that, I'd like to talk about imaginary moneys.

See, in my view, the world today is quite behind its potential, when it comes to money. It's now been, what?, 50 years since the advent of personal computers and 20 years since the mainstream adoption of the world wide web. And still, we''re transacting like hundreds of years ago.

But the world has changed. In fact, it changed already in the 70ies, when we found ourselves in the information age, forming the information society. Just look at how smart these people on the street of New York were 50 years ago.

Isn't it beautiful? To me, at least, it's almost like time-traveling and having a dialogue with the the collective of humans that were responsible for creating what we today call computers and the internet. It's inspiring, because these random strangers talk so clearly and freely about our world. It's so frightening, because today, at least I'd argue, we're witnessing their utopia as our dystopia.

It ties nicely into Ada Lovelace's experience of climbing the imaginary stairs too. The more we let our imagination go, the longer we fall. The more harder the impact? When actually, I think there's one particular climb we should take, no matter how great the risks of falling. The stairs of imaginary moneys.

Tim. What the fuck are you talking about??? Okokok. Hold on. Hear me out! Just one. One last story and then I'll explain.

Sooo, in the 2000s the www was really cool. And everybody bought stocks and all nerds got rich and it was great. It was so great that even traditional or established business wanted to go full cyber by hosting their own websites. One of them was the New York Times, an organization founded a staggering 150 years earlier.

Now, back in the days publishing websites was quite difficult. There were hardly any tools. I guess there weren't many developers. But I guess most importantly people were really stuck in a loop of Hypernormalization. That is, they didn't know what to do with the internet. And so it happened that the New York Times simply decided to scan their newspaper and upload it daily as an image on their website. They were so proud, they even published an article about themselves, quoting a consultant: "The market is booming for newspapers on the World Wide Web."

Little did they know... that you can't just scan your print edition and upload a picture to the website, because as McLuhan had realized years before already: "The medium is the message". And hence, the closest resemblence of a website cyberspace has created may be something like Twitter, Mastodon or Medium. That market truly is booming.

And that my fellow earthlings, is the epic saga of Hypernormalizations birth and death, as it too can be overcome with a simple trick of the mind: Imagination.

Today, there's a particular topic that needs more imagination and that is, as I've said before, the concept of imaginary moneys. I think, we'd be fine in climbing the stairs. We're collectively and subliminally aware of transactional relationships and keeping ledgers in our brain. David Graeber's 5000 Years of debt certainly is proof enough. I don't think we'd fall far, as in our deerest need, fiat can be our friend again.

So then let's explore multi dimensional money and leave these real and boring concepts behind. You're talking about credentials and reputation on the blockchain? Well, I'm talking about about credentials acting as a base for your money as an exponent.

You're talking about quadratic voting and reputation schemes? Well; I'm talking about reputation as a multiplier for your base supply of money. You vote more than once? Well better have some stash under your matress, because now you've significantly decreased your spending power but increased your political reach.

I mean, just imagine the possibilities in cybespace, where the relationship of monetary supply and political influence couldn't possibly grow exponentially or lineraly. But instead, where we could limit them through governance by adjusting iii, in:

z=a+bi z = a + bi z=a+bi

where zzz being the individual's spending power, aaa being the individual's money supply and bbb being the e.g. the individual's political influence.

I know, it sounds like a long shot now. But I truly think we're not that far away from ideas like these being put into practical use. In many ways, stable coins are proof of this. So let's take another imaginary step :)
